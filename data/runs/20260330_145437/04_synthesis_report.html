Finance Department Process Documentation Report — Single-Site Underground Gold Mine

Executive Summary

This report synthesizes three detailed interviews with finance department staff at a single-site underground gold mine: David Motswari (Business Partner Finance, Underground Operations), Priya Desai (Accounts Payable & Procurement Coordinator), and James Kipchoge (Finance Systems Administrator & Analyst). The interviews provide strong coverage of Accounts Payable, Procurement, Cost Accounting, Management Reporting, Business Partnering, and the systems landscape, with partial coverage of Payroll, Fixed Assets, Month-End Close, and Inventory processes. Key structural findings include a heavy reliance on spreadsheet-based workarounds to bridge gaps between disconnected source systems (Ellipse ERP, MineRP, Microster, Deswik), significant key-person dependencies — particularly around David Motswari's operational knowledge and Priya Desai's compensating controls — and a recurring pattern of upstream coding errors that propagate uncorrected through the transaction chain. The function is operationally effective due to individual competence and informal controls, but structurally fragile and well-suited for targeted automation and process improvement analysis.

David Motswari operates as the bridge between underground operations and central finance. Section 2: Process Domain Summaries

Accounts Payable

Coverage: Well-documented (Priya Desai — primary owner; David Motswari — contributor for verification)

Invoices arrive via shared email inbox (digital and scanned paper). Priya sorts, identifies PO numbers, and initiates three-way matching (PO, goods receipt, invoice) in Ellipse. Matching is semi-manual: Ellipse checks header-level value tolerance (~2% or $50) but line-by-line comparison is performed manually across screens. Goods receipt confirmation is a frequent bottleneck — 2-3 day delays waiting for operational staff to enter receipts. Priya maintains a parallel tracking spreadsheet and physical paper pack process because ERP approval notifications are unreliable. Payment runs occur weekly (Thursday), prepared from Wednesday afternoon. Priya manually reviews the payment proposal, verifies bank details, resolves holds, and presents the batch to Margaret for authorization. Remittance advices are sent post-payment in vendor-specific formats.

Key vulnerabilities: No-PO invoices (~35% of problematic invoices require investigation); dual approval tracking (ERP + paper); receipt confirmation delays; bank detail verification is manual.

---

Procurement and Purchase Order Management

Coverage: Well-documented (Priya Desai — processing; James Kipchoge — system design; David Motswari — contractor context)

Procurement is Ellipse's strongest area. Requisitions are raised by operational staff, routed through approval workflow, and converted to POs. However, cost coding happens at requisition stage by operational staff with no finance review before PO creation. Approval workflow validates authority and budget existence but not coding accuracy. Errors propagate through the full chain (PO → receipt → invoice → ledger) and are only caught at month-end review. Contractor procurement involves additional complexity: David Motswari informally verifies contractor work completion, creating a control concentration where the same person provides both cost estimates and receipt confirmations.

---

Cost Accounting and Allocation

Coverage: Well-documented (James Kipchoge — system mechanics; David Motswari — operational application)

Cost structure in Ellipse uses three dimensions: cost centre (organisational unit), account code (nature of expenditure), and work order (specific job/project). Work order usage is inconsistent — maintenance uses it well; mining development does not, relying instead on cost centre alone. This limits the ability to track cost-per-end or cost-per-activity within Ellipse.

Mining-to-processing cost allocation is performed monthly by Amara in a complex multi-tab spreadsheet. Inputs are Ellipse cost centre totals (which may carry unresolved coding errors). Allocation drivers and their currency (static vs. updated monthly) are unknown to James and need confirmation. Output feeds management accounts; whether it journals back into Ellipse is unconfirmed. This is a single-point-of-failure process — Amara is the sole maintainer.

30-60 recode journals are processed monthly to correct upstream coding errors. Correction workflow: Amara identifies errors in Excel cost reviews → investigates source in Ellipse → chases originator → prepares journal in spreadsheet template → entry into Ellipse → approval by Margaret above a threshold (threshold unconfirmed). No feedback loop exists to originators, so errors recur.

---

Management Reporting

Coverage: Partially documented (David Motswari — variance commentary; James Kipchoge — data extraction)

Cost centre reports are generated from Ellipse/Cognos, exported to Excel for analysis. David writes monthly variance commentary in Word documents covering all underground cost centres — explaining budget vs. actual, distinguishing timing variances from real overruns, and flagging accrual treatments. Commentary is distributed via email to Margaret and Johan. It is not stored in a shared system — it lives in David's sent folder, local machine, and a physical lever arch file. Commentary is the key to interpreting what is estimated vs. confirmed in cost reports, making it a critical key-person dependency.

David maintains a comprehensive Excel tracker ("the tracker") with tabs for dashboard (RAG-rated summary per development end), cost detail, survey reconciliation, issues log, and contractor cost tracking. RAG status is set by manual judgment using an unformalized ~15% threshold rule. The tracker has four years of history and is the primary operational intelligence tool for underground cost management. It exists only on David's laptop.

---

Business Partnering and Business Case Support

Coverage: Partially documented (David Motswari — primary)

David builds cost comparisons in Excel when engineers bring operational problems requiring expenditure decisions. For items under $250K, Johan and Themba approve via email; David documents and updates the budget. Over $250K or involving new contract scope, a formal memo is prepared, Margaret reviews the numbers, and the case goes to the Capex and Projects Committee. Approvals are stored in David's Outlook folder ("Approvals") — not in SAP, not on a shared drive. David's involvement is essential for credibility: "If it doesn't have my fingerprints on it, they won't believe it."

---

Payroll and Labour Cost Processing

Coverage: Partially documented (James Kipchoge — system/data flow; operational process unclear)

Microster captures employee ID, hours, shift type, gross amount, and HR department code (not a finance cost centre). A mapping table translates department codes to finance cost centres — this table exists as a shared spreadsheet with two versions (finance and HR), no authoritative system-of-record, and no formal protocol for updates when org structure changes. The extract is weekly, worked in Excel, then imported/journaled into Ellipse. No validation at import checks whether cost centre assignments are correct — if the mapping is wrong, the journal posts silently to the wrong cost centre. Detection relies entirely on human pattern recognition at month-end. Labour is the largest single cost category and the allocation with the weakest systemic controls.

Budgeting and Forecasting

Coverage: Minimally documented

David references updating budgets when business cases are approved, and his tracker contains budget metres and costs per development end. James references budget existence checks in the procurement approval workflow. The full budgeting cycle, forecast process, and tools were not described in detail by any interviewee.

Month-End and Year-End Close

Elements described across interviews: accrual journals (David calls Margaret to agree treatment; Margaret's team posts), recode journals (Amara identifies and prepares; posted into Ellipse), cost allocation (Amara's spreadsheet), variance commentary (David's Word document). Wednesday ~3pm is an effective cut-off for AP items making the payment run. The full close checklist, timeline, reconciliation steps, and year-end specific procedures were not described.

Process 1: Accounts Payable — Invoice to Payment

Trigger: Invoice arrives in shared AP email inbox (or is scanned from post)

Frequency: Continuous intake; weekly payment run (Thursday)

| Step | Role/Swim Lane | Activity | System/Tool | Decision? | Output/Handoff |

|------|---------------|----------|-------------|-----------|----------------|

| 1 | Priya | Sort inbox: separate invoices from statements, quotes, queries | Outlook (shared inbox) | Yes — classify document type | Invoices moved to processing folder; non-invoices to query folder |

| 2 | Priya | Identify PO number on invoice | Invoice PDF | Yes — PO present? | If yes → Step 3. If no → Step 2a |

| 2a | Priya | Search Ellipse by vendor/date for matching PO; if none found, email originating department to identify owner | Ellipse, Outlook | Yes — can PO be located? | If found → Step 3. If not → invoice parked in 'Pending — Awaiting PO or Receipt' subfolder and logged in tracking spreadsheet |

| 3 | Priya | Pull up PO in Ellipse; verify goods receipt exists | Ellipse | Yes — receipt confirmed? | If yes → Step 4. If no → chase operational staff by email/phone (2-3 day typical delay) |

| 3a | Priya (or David informally) | For contractor invoices: Priya phones David to verify work completion if Themba unavailable | Phone, WhatsApp screenshots | Yes — David confirms? | David sends confirmation email to Priya; Priya receipts in Ellipse |

| 4 | Priya | Three-way match: compare PO, receipt note, and invoice line by line | Ellipse (header tolerance check) + manual PDF/screen comparison | Yes — within tolerance? | If match → Step 5. If discrepancy → investigate with vendor or requester |

| 5 | Priya | Code invoice (GL account, cost centre) and post in ERP | Ellipse | No | Invoice enters approval workflow; logged in Priya's tracking spreadsheet |

| 6 | Priya | If ERP notification unreliable: print invoice + PO summary, walk to approver or place in tray | Paper pack, physical delivery | No | Parallel approval tracking initiated |

| 7 | Approver (dept head / Johan / Margaret) | Review and approve invoice | Ellipse (if notification works) or physical signature on paper pack | Yes — approve? | Signed pack returned to Priya; or ERP approval recorded |

| 8 | Priya | Wednesday PM: pull payment proposal from ERP; review completeness, verify bank details, resolve holds | Ellipse, Excel (vendor bank detail list) | Yes — all items ready? | Final payment batch prepared |

| 9 | Margaret | Review and authorize payment batch | Ellipse | Yes — authorize? | Payment released |

| 10 | Priya | Send remittance advices to vendors in vendor-specific formats | Ellipse / Email | No | Payment cycle complete |

Section 4: Cross-Process Observations

System Limitations & Workarounds

  • No integration between MineRP (survey/technical) and Ellipse (finance): Development metres — the key physical driver for cost-per-metre analysis — must be manually reconciled via CSV extracts and David's Excel tracker. This is the single largest system gap affecting underground cost management.
  • No integration between Aspen (plant) and Ellipse: Mining-to-processing cost allocation happens entirely outside the ERP in a spreadsheet maintained by one person (Amara).
  • Microster-to-Ellipse mapping maintained in parallel spreadsheets with no authoritative version, no change protocol, and no import validation. Labour costs — the largest single cost category — can post silently to wrong cost centres.
  • ERP approval notifications unreliable, forcing Priya to maintain a parallel paper-based approval process. "The ERP workflow exists and I use it, but I can't rely on it alone if I have a deadline. So I have the parallel process as a backup." — Priya Desai
  • Ellipse line-by-line matching is inadequate: Three-way match is effectively manual, with Priya comparing PDFs against screens. System tolerance check operates at header level only.
  • Cognos reporting requires Excel export for any analytical work, reinforcing spreadsheet dependency.

Handoff Friction Points

  • Goods receipt confirmation delays (2-3 days typical) caused by operational staff not entering receipts promptly, cascading into missed payment runs and vendor relationship strain.
  • Survey data lag (~1 week) creates a persistent gap between costs booked and metres confirmed, requiring judgment-based accruals every month.
  • No-PO invoices require Priya to conduct detective work across departments — "There's no consequence for not raising a PO... So the behaviour doesn't change." — Priya Desai
  • Contractor receipt confirmation routes informally through David when Themba is unavailable, bypassing the intended control structure.

Data Quality & Availability Issues

  • Cost coding errors at requisition stage propagate uncorrected through the entire transaction chain. 30-60 recode journals monthly represent the correction effort. "The correction fixes the ledger but it doesn't fix the process that caused the error." — James Kipchoge
  • HR-to-finance cost centre mapping table has no single authoritative version; two teams maintain separate copies that drift out of sync on org changes.
  • Journal narrative fields are free-text and inconsistently populated, preventing systematic analysis of error patterns or root causes.
  • Variance commentary — the key to understanding what is estimated vs. confirmed in cost reports — lives only in David's email, local machine, and a physical file.

Key-Person Dependencies

  • David Motswari holds the operational intelligence layer for underground cost management. His tracker (4 years of history), his variance commentary, his business case approvals, his contractor verifications, and his accrual judgments all reside in personal systems (laptop, Outlook, lever arch file). "If I got hit by a truck tomorrow, they'd be in trouble. I know that." — David Motswari
  • Amara is the sole maintainer of the mining-to-processing cost allocation spreadsheet — described by James as a single point of failure.
  • Priya Desai maintains compensating controls (tracking spreadsheet, vendor preference list, bank detail verification process, paper pack system) that exist nowhere else and are entirely undocumented.

High Manual Effort Areas

  • Invoice three-way matching: line-by-line manual comparison across screens/PDFs (50-70 invoices/week)
  • Tracking spreadsheet maintenance: Priya's AP log and David's underground tracker both require continuous manual update from multiple sources
  • Monthly recode journals: 30-60 correction entries requiring investigation, preparation, entry, and approval
  • Survey-to-cost reconciliation: manual CSV import, tracker update, and discrepancy investigation
  • Labour cost mapping: weekly manual translation of HR department codes to finance cost centres
  • Payment run preparation: manual review of proposal, bank detail verification, hold resolution

Improvement Opportunities Identified by Staff

  • Priya: Retrospective PO form with required explanation field to create friction and reduce no-PO invoices; fix ERP approval notifications to eliminate need for parallel paper process
  • James: Structured/enforced journal narrative field (low implementation cost) to enable miscoding pattern analysis; single authoritative mapping table for labour cost centre assignments with formal change protocol; validation rule at labour cost import to flag unknown or recently changed cost centres
  • David: Acknowledged need for knowledge documentation but no specific solution proposed; recognized his tracker should be shared or systematized
  • James (observation, not recommendation): Debit-credit patterns in recode journals are extractable and could be queried as a proxy for miscoding volume if narrative discipline were improved
