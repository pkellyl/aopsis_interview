Research Brief

Context

organisation name

Toyota-scale US Auto OEM (anonymized)

industry

Automotive Manufacturing — Original Equipment Manufacturer

description

A large, mature US-based automotive OEM with deep lean/TPS-influenced culture. The supply chain function employs approximately 350 people across multiple sub-functions including procurement, supplier quality, logistics, production planning, inventory management, and supply chain strategy. The organization operates with formal governance cadences and structured decision-making processes typical of Japanese-influenced manufacturing culture adapted to US operations.

organisational dynamics
culture

Process-driven, lean methodology embedded, consensus-oriented but hierarchical. Strong emphasis on standardization and continuous improvement.

structure

Multi-layered with VP/Director/Manager/Specialist tiers across six core sub-functions. Regional plant-level supply chain teams report into central supply chain leadership.

sub functions
  • Procurement / Purchasing (direct and indirect materials)
  • Supplier Quality / Supplier Development
  • Logistics & Transportation (inbound and outbound)
  • Production Planning & Scheduling
  • Inventory Management / Warehousing
  • Supply Chain Strategy & Analytics
known tensions

None specifically identified. This is a diagnostic baseline exercise to understand the current state of decision-making.

Interview Objectives

research topic

Decision-making topology of the supply chain function: mapping who makes what decisions, in what forums, with what authority, tied to what KPIs.

key questions
  • What is this person's role and scope of responsibility?
  • Who do they report to and who reports to them?
  • What KPIs do they personally own or are accountable for?
  • What decisions do they make independently vs. escalate?
  • What recurring meetings do they attend?
  • What is the purpose of each meeting, who else attends, and what types of decisions are made there?
  • What are the key outputs or outcomes of those meetings?
  • How do decisions from meetings translate into action — who carries them out?
themes to probe
  • Formal decision authority vs. informal influence
  • KPI ownership clarity — sole owner vs. shared accountability
  • Meeting effectiveness — are decisions actually made or just discussed?
  • Cross-functional handoffs — where does supply chain decision-making intersect with manufacturing, engineering, finance?
  • Escalation pathways — what goes up and what gets resolved locally

Interview Output Structure

format

Structured, punchy summaries per interviewee — no more than 2 pages each

required topics
  • topic: Role Profile
    details: Title, sub-function, scope, reporting line (up and down)
  • topic: KPI Ownership
    details: List of KPIs owned or co-owned, how they are measured, frequency of review
  • topic: Decision Authority
    details: Key decisions this person makes, decision boundaries, what gets escalated and to whom
  • topic: Meeting Participation
    details: List of recurring meetings attended, their cadence, attendees, purpose, and types of decisions made in each
  • topic: Meeting Outcomes
    details: What outputs or actions come from each meeting, who is accountable for follow-through
depth criteria

Focused and factual. Prioritize structure and specificity over narrative. Each interview should produce data points that can be directly mapped onto the network diagram.

per interview deliverable

A structured role card with: role profile, KPI table, decision inventory, and meeting-by-meeting breakdown with attendees and decision types

Report Objectives

report type

Executive diagnostic brief with visual decision-making map

target audience

Top management and consulting team — people who need to quickly understand the current state and identify priorities for improving decision-making

output sections
  • section: Decision-Making Network Map
    description: Visual network diagram showing roles (nodes), meetings (nodes), KPIs (nodes), and the connections between them. Should show who attends what, who owns what, and where decision authority sits. Color-coded by sub-function and layered by seniority.
  • section: Meeting Architecture
    description: A structured overview of all core recurring meetings in the supply chain function: purpose, cadence, attendees by role, decision types, and outputs. Organized from strategic (quarterly/monthly) to operational (weekly/daily).
  • section: KPI Ownership Matrix
    description: A matrix mapping every identified KPI to its owner(s), the meetings where it is reviewed, and the decisions it drives.
  • section: Decision Authority Map
    description: A layered view showing what types of decisions are made at each level (VP, Director, Manager, Specialist) and in which forums — highlighting where authority is concentrated, distributed, or ambiguous.
  • section: Executive Narrative & Priorities
    description: A punchy 2-3 page written brief summarizing: how decisions actually flow in the supply chain, where the system is clear and well-governed, where there are potential gaps, overlaps, or bottlenecks in decision-making, and a prioritized list of areas to investigate further for improvement.
focus areas
  • Clarity of decision ownership across the function
  • Meeting effectiveness — are the right people in the right rooms making the right decisions?
  • KPI alignment — do KPIs drive the right decisions at the right level?
  • Cross-functional seams — where supply chain decision-making depends on or conflicts with adjacent functions
  • Structural efficiency — is the meeting and governance architecture proportionate or bloated?
