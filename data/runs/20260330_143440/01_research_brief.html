Research Brief

Context

organisation
name

Single-Site Gold Mine (Underground)

industry

Mining — Gold, Underground

size

Approximately 600 employees on site

operations

Standard underground development, production stoping, and surface plant processing for gold doré production

description

A single-site underground gold mining operation with a centralised finance function supporting all operational and administrative finance needs. The finance department comprises approximately 20 people and covers all finance processes except corporate finance. The team is split between on-site business partners embedded in operational areas (development, production, plant, engineering, safety) and a central finance team handling transactional, reporting, compliance, and planning processes.

finance team structure
on site business partners
description

Finance business partners embedded in operational departments. Typically one per major operational area. Focused on cost management, budget tracking, business case development, and operational financial support.

likely areas
  • Underground Development
  • Underground Production
  • Plant Processing
  • Engineering and Maintenance
  • Health, Safety and Environment (support role)
profile

Operationally oriented, close to cost drivers, often longer-tenured site employees with strong relationships with department heads. May use informal processes alongside formal systems.

central finance team
description

Core finance function handling transactional processing, financial reporting, planning, compliance, payroll, and systems management. Physically or organisationally separate from the operational areas.

likely roles
  • Finance Manager / Controller
  • Management Accountant(s)
  • Accounts Payable Clerk(s)
  • Accounts Receivable / Revenue Accountant
  • Payroll Administrator(s)
  • Procurement / Purchase Order Administrator
  • Budget and Forecasting Analyst
  • Fixed Assets Accountant
  • Financial Reporting Accountant
  • Tax and Compliance Officer
  • Systems / ERP Administrator
profile

Mix of experienced long-tenured staff with deep institutional knowledge and newer staff. May follow established routines with limited process questioning. Central team may experience tension with site partners around data quality, cost allocations, and reporting timeliness.

known dynamics
site vs central tension

Likely divergence in priorities — site partners focused on operational speed and cost control, central team focused on accuracy, compliance, and deadlines. May result in duplicate work, informal workarounds, or data reconciliation issues.

tenure mix

Expectation of long-tenured employees with deep but undocumented knowledge alongside newer staff. Long-tenured staff may have normalised inefficiencies; newer staff may have fresh perspectives on gaps.

automation sensitivity

Some staff may be cautious about the implications of process documentation for job security. Interview approach should frame the exercise as process improvement and value creation, not headcount reduction.

system landscape

Typical for this type of operation to run an ERP (e.g., SAP, Oracle, or a mining-specific system like Pronto or JD Edwards) alongside spreadsheet-heavy workarounds, especially in reporting and forecasting.

process scope
included
  • Accounts Payable
  • Accounts Receivable and Revenue Recognition
  • Payroll and Labour Cost Processing
  • Procurement and Purchase Order Management
  • Cost Accounting and Allocation
  • Management Reporting
  • Budgeting and Forecasting
  • Fixed Assets and Capital Expenditure
  • Financial Close (Month-End and Year-End)
  • Tax and Statutory Compliance

excluded
  • Corporate Finance

  • M&A and investor relations

Interview Objectives

research topic

Documentation of finance department processes at a single-site underground gold mine, capturing sufficient detail per role to enable end-to-end process flow construction and subsequent identification of automation, agentic AI, and value creation opportunities.

primary goal

Capture each individual's participation in finance processes in structured, consistent detail — including triggers, inputs, activities, outputs, systems used, handoffs, and decision points — to enable consolidation into complete process flow diagrams.

secondary goal

Opportunistically capture any pain points, inefficiencies, manual workarounds, or frustrations that surface naturally during the conversation, without making this the primary focus.

key questions
  • What processes or process steps does this person own, participate in, or support?
  • What triggers each process or task — what event, schedule, or request initiates the work?
  • What are the inputs to each process — data, documents, requests, system outputs?
  • What activities does the person perform — step by step?
  • What systems, tools, or templates are used at each step?
  • What are the outputs — reports, entries, approvals, files, communications?
  • Who receives those outputs, and what do they do with them?
  • Where do handoffs occur — to whom, in what form, and when?
  • Where are approvals or decisions required, and who makes them?
  • What are the timing and frequency of each process?
  • Are there known exceptions, edge cases, or variations in how the process runs?
  • What happens when something goes wrong — what is the error or exception handling path?
opportunistic themes
  • Manual or repetitive steps the interviewee finds time-consuming
  • Points of frustration, rework, or delay
  • Workarounds used because the system or process doesn't fully support the need
  • Data quality or availability issues
  • Dependency on specific individuals (key-person risk)
  • Steps the interviewee believes could be done better or faster

Interview Output Structure

description

Each interview must produce a structured process participation record for that individual, formatted consistently to enable cross-interview consolidation into end-to-end process flows.

per interview deliverables
role profile
description

Brief description of the interviewee's role, team location (site or central), and primary area of responsibility.

fields
  • Role title
  • Team (on-site business partner or central finance)
  • Operational area supported (if applicable)
  • Approximate tenure or experience level (junior / mid / senior)
process participation inventory
description

A list of all processes or process segments this person participates in, named consistently using a standard process taxonomy.

fields
  • Process name (standardised)
  • Process area / domain
  • This person's role in the process (owner, contributor, approver, recipient)
  • Frequency (daily, weekly, monthly, ad hoc, event-triggered)
process detail records
description

For each process or process segment identified, a structured detail record capturing enough information to map the process step by step.

fields per process
  • Process name
  • Trigger: what starts this process
  • Inputs: data, documents, system outputs, or requests required
  • Steps performed by this person: sequential list of activities
  • Systems and tools used at each step
  • Decision points: where judgement or approval is required and by whom
  • Outputs produced: reports, entries, approvals, communications, files
  • Handoffs: who receives the output, in what format, via what channel
  • Timing and deadlines
  • Known exceptions or variations
  • Exception handling: what happens when errors or problems occur
noted observations
description

Any pain points, inefficiencies, workarounds, or improvement ideas that surfaced naturally during the interview. Captured verbatim or as close paraphrase. Not solicited but recorded if offered.

fields
  • Process reference
  • Observation or comment
  • Nature of observation (manual effort, delay, data issue, workaround, key-person dependency, other)
depth criteria
minimum acceptable

Each step in a process can be understood by someone unfamiliar with the role. Triggers, inputs, outputs, and handoffs are all explicitly captured. Systems used are named.

target depth

Sufficient detail to draw a swim-lane flow diagram for each process segment without needing to return to the interviewee for clarification.

consistency requirement

All interviews use the same process naming taxonomy and field structure so outputs can be directly compared and merged.

interview approach notes
framing

Position the exercise as capturing institutional knowledge to improve and future-proof the finance function — not as a job evaluation or headcount exercise.

tone

Conversational and curious. Let the interviewee describe their work in their own words first, then use structured follow-up questions to fill in the required fields.

handling long tenure

For long-tenured staff, probe gently for steps that have become automatic or assumed — these are often undocumented and critical.

handling newer staff

Newer staff may have useful observations about where processes are unclear or inefficient — note these as observations.

handling sensitivity

If automation anxiety surfaces, acknowledge it without dismissing it. Refocus on the value of making their expertise visible and their work easier.

Report Objectives

report type

Process Documentation and Flow Synthesis Report

target audience

Internal analysts, process improvement leads, or technology/automation consultants who will subsequently conduct pain point mapping and identify automation and agentic AI opportunities.

report purpose

To consolidate individual interview outputs into a complete, accurate, and visually navigable map of the finance department's actual operating processes — as they are performed today — at a level of detail sufficient to support automation analysis.

output sections
  • section: Executive Summary
    content: Overview of scope, number of interviews conducted, process domains covered, and key structural observations about the finance function.
  • section: Finance Function Overview
    content: Description of the finance team structure, split between on-site business partners and central finance, roles covered, and systems landscape.
  • section: Process Domain Summaries
    content: For each major process domain (e.g., Accounts Payable, Payroll, Management Reporting, etc.), a narrative description of how the process operates end to end, who is involved, key decision points, and notable characteristics or vulnerabilities.
    domains to cover:
    • Accounts Payable
    • Accounts Receivable and Revenue
    • Payroll and Labour Cost Processing
    • Procurement and Purchase Order Management
    • Cost Accounting and Allocation
    • Management Reporting
    • Budgeting and Forecasting
    • Fixed Assets and Capital Expenditure
    • Month-End and Year-End Close
    • Tax and Statutory Compliance
    • Business Partnering and Business Case Support
    • Inventory and Stores Finance Interface
    • Cash Management (site-level)
    • Intercompany and Recharges
  • section: Process Flow Diagrams
    content: Swim-lane flow diagrams for each key process domain, showing the sequence of activities, decision points, system interactions, handoffs between roles, and inputs/outputs. Diagrams should be drawn at a level of detail that makes individual steps visible and assignable to a role or system. Format: swim lanes by role or team, with process steps, decision diamonds, and system annotations.
    diagram specifications:
    lane structure

    Swim lanes by role or functional team

    elements required
    • Process start and end points
    • Activity steps (labelled clearly)
    • Decision points with Yes/No or conditional branches
    • System or tool used at each step (annotated)
    • Handoff points between roles
    • Inputs entering the process
    • Outputs leaving the process
    • Timing or frequency indicators where relevant
    detail level

    Sufficient to identify individual automatable or agent-replaceable steps without further investigation

  • section: Cross-Process Observations
    content: Synthesised observations that cut across multiple processes — e.g., recurring system limitations, common handoff friction points, data flow gaps, key-person dependencies, and high-manual-effort areas. These are drawn from opportunistic captures during interviews and analyst observation, not from a formal pain point exercise.
  • section: Appendix: Individual Interview Summaries
    content: Structured summary per interviewee showing their role profile, process participation inventory, and process detail records. Serves as the evidentiary base for the flow diagrams and cross-process observations.
focus areas for subsequent use
  • Identification of high-volume, rules-based, repetitive process steps suitable for RPA or workflow automation
  • Identification of judgement-intensive or data-synthesis steps suitable for agentic AI transformation
  • Mapping of data flows to identify integration opportunities and system consolidation potential
  • Identification of key-person dependencies and knowledge concentration risks
  • Prioritisation of process improvement opportunities by effort, frequency, and value impact
