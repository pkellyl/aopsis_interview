On attendance from Lisa's team — it's typically one of her senior procurement managers, usually a person named Rachel Huang who covers the component categories most relevant to whatever program we're reviewing. Occasionally Lisa attends herself but that's not the norm. My honest read is that Lisa's team views these sessions as lower priority than their day-to-day supplier management work, which affects the level of engagement. On actual decisions from the past six months — I'd point to one session on our compact SUV program where we identified a bracket redesign that simplified assembly and eliminated a supplier-specific tooling requirement. That was a genuine win — cost reduction and improved manufacturability, identified early enough to execute cleanly. That's the kind of output these sessions are supposed to generate. But I'd struggle to give you a second concrete example from the past six months that resulted in a documented decision with clear follow-through. That's telling. On the quality variation question — a high-quality session has the right people in the room with authority to make decisions, engineering has specific design questions we need manufacturing or supply chain input on, and we leave with documented action items and owners. A low-quality session is essentially a presentation — engineering shows designs, attendees ask some questions, nothing gets decided, follow-up is vague. Those sessions happen because they're on the calendar, not because there's a specific problem to solve. And those are the majority, if I'm being honest with myself.
