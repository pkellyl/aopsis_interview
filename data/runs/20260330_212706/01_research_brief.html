Research Brief

Context

organization name

Toyota Motor North America – Supply Chain & Procurement Division (fictional/archetypal)

industry

Automotive Manufacturing (OEM)

description

A large-scale automotive OEM supply chain organization modeled on Toyota USA. The function covers direct and indirect procurement, supplier development, inbound and outbound logistics, production planning, inventory management, and S&OP. The organization operates across multiple manufacturing plants in the US (e.g., Kentucky, Texas, Indiana), with a central corporate supply chain office in Plano, TX. It interfaces heavily with global sourcing (Japan HQ), regional suppliers, third-party logistics providers, and internal functions including manufacturing, finance, and product engineering.

scale

Large OEM. ~$50B+ in annual procurement spend. Thousands of active suppliers. Multiple plants producing passenger vehicles, SUVs, and trucks.

known dynamics and tensions
  • Tension between centralized corporate procurement strategy and plant-level operational autonomy — plants often make local decisions that conflict with enterprise supplier agreements.
  • Generational divide between tenured employees trained in the Toyota Production System (TPS) philosophy and newer hires with data science / digital supply chain backgrounds who push for algorithmic decision-making.
  • Geography-based tension between the Plano TX corporate hub and manufacturing plant teams in Kentucky, Indiana, and Texas — plant teams feel distant from corporate decisions.
  • Persistent tension between supply chain and product engineering over lead times: engineering changes are introduced late and disrupt supplier schedules.
  • Post-COVID and post-semiconductor-shortage legacy: some resilience investments (dual sourcing, safety stock) are now seen as cost inefficiencies by finance, creating cross-functional friction.
  • Japan HQ influence: certain strategic sourcing decisions are made in Japan with limited North American input, creating a perceived lack of ownership and accountability in the US team.
  • S&OP process is a known fault line — sales/commercial forecasts are often not trusted by supply chain planners, leading to informal shadow forecasting.

Interview Objectives

research topic

How decisions are made within the Toyota USA supply chain organization and its adjacent functions — mapping decision ownership, information flows, meeting structures, KPIs, and reporting relationships to produce an organizational decision graph.

key questions
  • What decisions does this person own, influence, or get consulted on?
  • What information do they rely on to make decisions, and where does it come from?
  • What meetings do they attend, and what is their role in each?
  • What are their KPIs and how do those KPIs shape their decision-making priorities?
  • Who do they report to, and who reports to them?
  • Where do they feel decisions are slow, unclear, or contested?
  • Where do they feel disconnected from decisions that affect their work?
  • How do they interact with adjacent functions (manufacturing, finance, engineering, commercial)?
  • What happens when something goes wrong — who decides, and how fast?
themes to probe
  • Decision ownership vs. decision influence — who formally decides vs. who really drives outcomes
  • Meeting culture — which meetings produce decisions vs. which are informational or performative
  • KPI conflicts — where one person's KPI works against another's
  • Information asymmetry — who has visibility and who is flying blind
  • Escalation paths — how problems travel up and across the organization
  • Informal power — whose opinion carries weight beyond their title
  • Speed and bottlenecks — where decisions get stuck
  • Plant vs. corporate divide
  • HQ (Japan) vs. North America decision rights

Interview Output Structure

required topics per interview
  • Reporting line: who this person reports to (name/title of direct manager)
  • Direct reports: who reports to this person (titles and count)
  • KPIs: 3–6 specific KPIs this person is measured on, with a note on how each shapes behavior
  • Meetings: list of recurring meetings attended, with frequency, role in each (lead / participant / reviewer), and what decisions or outputs each meeting produces
  • Decision inventory: list of decisions this person owns or co-owns, categorized by type (strategic / operational / transactional) and frequency (daily / weekly / monthly / ad hoc)
  • Key interfaces: which other roles or functions this person interacts with most, and the nature of each interaction (provides input / receives input / joint decision / approval)
  • Pain points: where this person experiences friction, delay, confusion, or misalignment in decision-making
  • Workarounds: informal practices this person uses to get things done that bypass formal processes
depth criteria
  • Each interview should be rich enough to place the person precisely in the organizational graph — their node, their edges, and the direction/weight of those edges
  • KPIs must be specific and realistic — not generic (e.g., not just 'cost savings' but 'year-over-year PPV reduction target of 3% on direct materials')
  • Meetings must be named and described specifically enough to identify overlaps and gaps across interviews
  • Decision inventory must distinguish between decisions this person believes they own and decisions where ownership is contested or unclear
  • Pain points should be expressed in the person's voice — specific, grounded, not abstract
per interview deliverables
  • Structured profile covering all required topics above
  • A short narrative (150–200 words) describing how this person experiences decision-making in the organization — their perspective, frustrations, and workarounds
  • A node-and-edge summary: a simple list of connections (this person → meeting / KPI / decision / role) suitable for ingestion into a graph visualization tool

Report Objectives

report type

Organizational decision-flow diagnostic — a synthesis of all interviews into a connected graph and interpretive analysis, designed for top management and external consultants.

target audience

Chief Supply Chain Officer, VP-level supply chain and operations leaders, and consulting team conducting organizational diagnostic.

output sections
  • section: Executive Summary
    description: 2–3 page summary of the most significant findings about how decisions are made, where the major fault lines are, and the highest-priority areas for diagnostic follow-up.
  • section: Organizational Decision Graph
    description: A visual and structured map showing: all roles as nodes; edges representing reporting lines, meeting co-attendance, shared KPIs, and decision interdependencies; annotated with decision types and frequency. Should be renderable in a graph tool (e.g., nodes/edges exported as structured data).
  • section: Decision Inventory
    description: A consolidated table of all significant decisions identified across interviews, showing: decision name, primary owner, co-owners/influencers, frequency, meeting where it occurs (if any), and KPIs it affects.
  • section: Meeting Map
    description: A catalog of all recurring meetings identified, showing: attendees, frequency, decision outputs, and how each meeting connects to the broader decision flow. Identifies meetings that are overdense (too many decisions), underdense (no real decisions), or duplicative.
  • section: KPI Conflict Analysis
    description: An analysis of where KPIs across roles are in tension or contradiction — e.g., where a procurement KPI incentivizes behavior that damages a logistics or manufacturing KPI.
  • section: Fault Line Analysis
    description: Identification of the key organizational fault lines — plant vs. corporate, function vs. function, HQ vs. North America, formal vs. informal authority — with evidence from interviews and the decision graph.
  • section: Opportunity Zones
    description: Hypothesis-free summary of areas where the decision graph reveals structural gaps, bottlenecks, ownership ambiguity, or misalignment — framed as questions for further diagnosis rather than prescriptive recommendations.
  • section: Appendix: Individual Interview Profiles
    description: Full structured profiles for each of the 13 interviewees, including node-and-edge summaries.
focus areas
  • Decision ownership clarity vs. ambiguity
  • Speed and bottlenecks in the decision flow
  • KPI alignment and conflict across the organization
  • Formal vs. informal power and decision-making
  • Cross-functional and cross-geography disconnects
  • Meeting efficiency and decision-producing capacity
  • Resilience and responsiveness of the decision structure to disruption
