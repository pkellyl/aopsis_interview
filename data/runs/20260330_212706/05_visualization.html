Organizational Decision-Flow Diagnostic Report

Toyota Motor North America – Supply Chain & Procurement Division

13 Interviews · Supply Chain, Procurement, Manufacturing, Finance, Engineering, Analytics · Plano TX + Plant Locations
Critical Finding: The most significant fault line is a structural misalignment between cost-reduction KPIs (Finance/Procurement) and supply resilience investments (Supply Chain leadership), distorting decisions on semiconductor dual-sourcing and safety stock with no forum currently equipped to resolve it. A secondary fault line between corporate Plano and Georgetown KY has produced de facto plant-level sourcing authority tacitly endorsed by VP Manufacturing but never formalized. The S&OP process is functionally compromised by documented shadow forecasting, and decision ownership is contested or ambiguous across at least 7 of 14 high-stakes decision domains. Highest-priority follow-up: the governance gap between VP Supply Chain and COO where multiple unresolved cross-functional decisions await executive sponsorship.

Decision Ownership Status
Only 7 of 14 significant decisions have clear, named owners. The remaining 7 are either contested between two parties or simply unassigned — an unusually high ambiguity rate for an organization of this scale.
Meeting Classification by Decision-Producing Capacity
Only 5 of 13 mapped meetings reliably produce decisions. Three are "performative" or defunct — running on cadence without outputs. The Executive S&OP is overdense: too many decisions competing for too little accountability structure.

The Supply Resilience Working Group (defunct since 2022) is the clearest structural gap: it was the only cross-functional forum with a mandate to govern proactive supplier risk. Its dissolution leaves supplier risk governance absorbed but unresolved across S&OP and commodity reviews that are not designed for it.
KPI Conflict Analysis
Seven identified KPI pairs where one person's performance metric directly undermines another's. Severity rated High / Medium.
The most systemic pattern is the asymmetry between hard cost KPIs (PPV %, cost realization %, carrying cost %) on the Finance/Procurement axis versus soft directional risk indicators (resilience score, supply risk score) on the Supply Chain side. In any resource conversation, hard numbers win by default — meaning resilience arguments lose structurally, not on merit.
